The Great Art of Ramon Llull — An Interactive Instrument for Combining Truth
Ramon Llull · Ars Brevis, 1308

In the year 1308, the Majorcan philosopher Ramon Llull completed the Ars Brevis — a condensed version of his lifelong project, the Ars Magna or "Great Art." This was not merely a text to be read, but a machine for generating knowledge: a combinatorial device that could produce propositions, questions, and arguments by mechanically combining a set of fundamental principles. Llull believed these principles — divine attributes like Goodness, Greatness, and Wisdom — were the atoms of all reality, and that their combinations would unlock universal truth.

The Nine Absolute Principles (Figure A)

The first figure contains nine dignities — attributes of God that Llull considered the fundamental predicates of all being. Each is assigned a letter. Every proposition generated by the Art involves at least one of these principles as subject or predicate.

How to Use this Instrument

The Ars Brevis operates through four figures. The Fourth Figure — a set of three rotating concentric circles — is the combinatorial engine. It generates every possible trio of letters (B through K), each letter standing for one of the nine absolute principles. Each combination of three letters generates a compartment: a proposition, question, and argumentation framework.

Navigate to The Fourth Figure tab to interact with the volvelle. Drag the rings to rotate them, then see the proposition generated. Navigate to The Table to see Llull's complete matrix of all 84 ternary combinations. Use Print & Assemble to create a physical paper volvelle.

Folio II · The Fourth Figure · Figura Quarta

The Table (Tabula) of the Ars Brevis lists all 84 possible ternary combinations of the nine letters B through K, without repetition of any letter in a given triplet. Llull arranged these into columns, each headed by a pair of letters that anchors the combination. Click any cell to see its meaning.

Folio III · The Table · Tabula

The volvelle below is designed to be printed, cut, and assembled with a brass brad (paper fastener) through the center. Print on cardstock for best results. The three concentric rings spin independently to generate the 84 ternary combinations of Llull's alphabet.

Assembly Instructions

  1. Print this page at 100% scale on cardstock (recommended) or heavy paper
  2. Carefully cut out all three circles along the outer edge
  3. Stack the circles from largest (bottom) to smallest (top), aligning the center holes
  4. Push a brass brad through all three center holes to hold them together
  5. Spread the brad tabs flat on the back — the rings should rotate freely
  6. Use the index pointer at the top to read the current combination
